## Context Setting

Prior to this A/B test, what were the baseline viewing times and completion rates for the original 50-word slide format?

Have there been any previous attempts to modify the slide word count or format, and if so, what were the outcomes of those changes?

Has any direct feedback been collected from employees regarding the "cognitive fatigue" or engagement levels of the original 50-word slides?

What specific data cleaning or outlier removal protocols were used in previous analyses to ensure consistency with the current pipeline?


## Goal Alignment 

While we are measuring viewing time, is the ultimate goal to increase engagement time, or to optimize it for better knowledge retention?

What is the minimum threshold for a "meaningful effect" in viewing time (e.g., a specific number of minutes) that would justify a company-wide rollout of the 15-word format?

How should we weigh a higher average viewing time against the risk that users are spending more time simply because the 15-word slides are too vague?

Are there secondary goals, such as reducing the number of users who drop off early or ensuring a higher "floor" for baseline attention?


## Define Over-performance 

If viewing times for the 15-word slides are significantly higher than the 50-word slides, at what point does "high engagement" become "inefficient learning"? Is there a time threshold that suggests users are struggling with the content rather than enjoying it?

If the treatment group shows nearly 100% completion rates, should we consider immediately pivoting all existing NotGPT workshops to the 15-word format, or is this effect specific to this particular subject matter?

Did the treatment group exhibit fewer "rewinds" or "re-watches"? If the streamlined format led to high engagement and lower navigation friction, how does that change our definition of a successful slide deck?


## Data Sourcing

Can we join this viewing time data with quiz results or final exam scores to see if the 15-word slide group actually retained more information?

Does the effectiveness of the 15-word format vary based on the user's professional background or prior experience with the topic?

Do we have access to "per-slide" viewing data? Knowing exactly which slide caused a user to drop off would provide much more granular insight than a total session time.

Can we source qualitative data (e.g., Likert scales on "ease of use") to see if the users' perceived effort matches the actual time spent on the slides?

What devices were used (mobile vs. desktop)? A 15-word slide might perform significantly better on mobile screens compared to the 50-word version.
